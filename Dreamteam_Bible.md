## Series Treatment — The Dreamteam Chronicles: Festival of Shadows (Firedog Edition)

Refer to `Dreamteam_Master_Dossier.md` for the consolidated overview. This treatment captures the 5-issue arc with the beer reveal in Issue #1, retaining ambiguity, and integrating storm, silent disco, food trucks, hammocks, vendor pop-ups, and the Man in Red as catalyst.

A 20-friend convoy spends five days at Firedog Festival in The Woodlands, Delaware. The official biergarten beer appears to corrupt attendees while a flamboyant Red Guy’s LSD reveals an uncanny layer of reality to the five-person Dreamteam. The team must neutralize the biergarten’s influence and still make Paul McCartney’s closing set.

- Source note: "2 stories one book" and "Personal feelings and memories and emotions like an autobiography between pages of graphics and superheroes. Two separate worlds or two in the same. Same emotions feelings and everything just different content."

### Approach
- Interleave autobiographical reflections (Adam’s perspective: feelings, memories, real festival beats) between graphic/superhero sequences (heightened, psychedelic confrontations and missions).
- Maintain parallel emotional arcs: the same emotions and truths are expressed in both modes, though the content and imagery differ.

1) Festival map and setlist
2) Group photo
3) Cars packed and driving to Firedog

### POV Discipline
- Autobiographical pages: cleaner layouts, grounded visuals, minimal distortion.
- Superhero pages: stylized, psychedelic overlays, surreal motifs, ambiguous threats.

## 5. Canon & Perception

### File: docs\canon\Canon_Rules.md

## Canon Rules — Perception, Communication, Ambiguity

- Perception while high:
  - Psychedelics: people appear magical/caricatured (“true self”).
  - Sober: appear normal.
  - Festival beer drunk: appear as zombies/monsters.
- Communication while high:
  - Can communicate almost psychically with other high people.
  - Cannot communicate with sober or drunk people — exceptions: vendors/cashiers behind counters and Matt.
- Telepathy: near-psychic coordination among trippers; used for rescue/strategy/comedy.
- Ambiguity:
  - No omniscient villain scenes; all evil filtered through Dreamteam’s POV.
  - Fleeting “behind-the-scenes” glimpses must remain interpretable as real or hallucination.

### Implementation Notes (for scripts/art)
- POV Switches:
  - Dreamteam POV: trippy overlays, stylized eyes/ooze effects, warped panel borders.
  - Matt POV: clean lines, normal palettes, no distortions.
- Vendors Exception:
  - Dialogue with vendors/cashiers renders normally even when characters are high.
- Biergarten Illusions:
  - Any keg/eye/ooze motif should always be deniable via a Matt POV beat within 1–2 pages.

- Night one Molly water in the thicket triggers perception shift: see what is happening to the people and the true nature of staff/surroundings.

  - "Saw the mad hatter as magical."

- General rule from notes: everything looks normal until drugs taken; then:
  - People on drugs: true self, magical creatures/caricatures.
  - Sober: normal.

- Use panel tags and captions to indicate evolving understanding across issues.

## 11. Comic Script Format

### File: scripts\format\Comic_Script_Format.md

## Comic Script Format — Guide

- Page heading: `PAGE N (Panels)`
- Panel heading: `Panel X:` concise action, key visual focus.
- Dialogue: `CHARACTER: text` (keep balloons short).
- Captions: `CAPTION (Name): text`
- SFX: `SFX: text`
- Art notes: camera, mood, motion cues when essential.

### POV Convention (This Project)
- Dreamteam POV: allow trippy overlays and stylized cues in panel notes.
- Matt POV: explicitly note “Matt POV” for clean, normal rendering.

### Sample
PAGE 1 (5 panels)
Panel 1: Wide shot. Convoy enters The Woodlands.
CAPTION (Adam): Five days. Twenty friends. One legendary set.
Panel 2: Campsite chaos, tents half-pitched.
Nick: I got the stakes—wait, these are chopsticks.
Panel 3: Food trucks line. Sam points to map.
Sam: If we sprint, we can still catch the opener.
Panel 4: Biergarten banner flaps. Staffer stares blankly.
Dani: Do you feel that?
Panel 5: Red Guy’s card in a hand: “Call me!”
CAPTION (Adam): And then it got weird.

Before diving into writing the full script for a comic, movie, or book, creators often follow a few “pre-production” or “development” steps to clarify their ideas and ensure everyone is on the same page. The specifics can vary depending on the medium and the team, but here are some common steps:

1. Brainstorming & Conceptualizing

Idea Dump: Start by jotting down every idea you have—characters, themes, plot points, setting details, visuals, etc. Don’t worry about organization yet.

Core Premise: Identify the central conflict or “hook” (e.g., “A group of friends at a festival discover a hidden evil”).

2. Research & Inspiration

Gather References: Look at similar works (comics, films, novels) for inspiration on tone, style, and structure.

Subject Matter Research: If the story involves specific cultural or historical elements (like festival culture, psychedelics, subcultures), read up on them or talk to people who are experts.

3. High-Level Outline or “Treatment”

Synopsis: Write a short paragraph (or page) summarizing the story from beginning to end—who the main characters are, what challenges they face, and how it all resolves.

Story Beats: List out major events or “beats” (e.g., “Meet the characters,” “The Red Guy appears,” “They discover tainted beer,” etc.). This ensures you have a roadmap.

4. Character Profiles

Bios & Motivations: Write a brief description for each main character: backstory, personality traits, goals, and how they change over the course of the story.

Relationships: Note how characters connect with each other (e.g., best friends, romantic partners, rivals).

5. Visual and Tonal References (especially for a comic or film)

Mood Boards: Gather images, color palettes, or even music that matches the tone you want.

Art Style & Setting: If it’s a comic, define the art style (cartoony, realistic, manga-inspired, etc.). If it’s a film, think about how it might be shot (dark and moody or bright and surreal).

6. Pilot Scene / Sample Pages

Test Pages: Write (or draw, in the case of a comic) a short sequence to get a feel for the tone, pacing, and dialogue. This can be used to show others how the project might look and feel before fully committing.

7. Pitch Materials (if collaborating or seeking funding)

Pitch Deck or Bible: A concise document (sometimes with visuals) that explains the concept, main characters, key themes, and potential for expansion.

Elevator Pitch: A quick one-liner or two about your project that grabs attention (e.g., “A group of festival-goers gains psychedelic powers to battle a hidden evil corrupting the crowd.”).

8. Feedback & Collaboration

Share with Trusted Peers: Before finalizing, you might ask friends or co-creators for feedback on the outline or sample pages.

Revise Based on Notes: Incorporate useful feedback, clarify plot holes, tighten character arcs.

9. Finalizing the “Blueprint”

Detailed Outline / Script Breakdown: Once the outline is solid, expand each bullet point or scene into a more detailed roadmap. This could be a panel-by-panel breakdown for a comic or a scene-by-scene outline for a film or novel.

Production Plan: For a comic, you might detail how many issues or chapters there will be, how you’ll schedule artwork, editing, and printing. For a film, you create a shooting schedule and budget estimates.

10. Full Script Writing

Drafting: With all the groundwork done, you start writing the full script (or, in the case of a comic, scripting pages/panels).

Revision Cycles: Expect multiple drafts as you refine dialogue, pacing, and visuals.

Why These Steps?

Clarity & Consistency: Pre-production steps help you avoid major plot holes and keep character motivations consistent.

1. Pitch Deck / Pitch Bible
What It Is

What It Is

A detailed breakdown of each main and secondary character’s personality, background, motivations, relationships, and visual references.

Typical Contents

Visual Reference: Costume design, color palettes, hairstyle notes, body language references.

Relationships: Short paragraphs on how each character relates to others.

Why You Might Need It

Ensures consistency across writing and art. If multiple people (writer, penciler, colorist) are involved, they need a clear reference.

3. Scene-by-Scene Outline / Beat Sheet
What It Is

A more granular breakdown of your story—page by page or scene by scene—detailing the progression of events, conflicts, and reveals.

Typical Contents

Numbered Scenes: Each labeled with approximate page ranges in your issue.

Key Actions & Turning Points: Noting character entrances/exits, conflicts, or dramatic moments.

Emotional Beats: Quick notes on the tension or mood shifts.

Why You Might Need It

Bridges the gap between your treatment and the full script.

4. Full Script (Comic Script Format)
What It Is

The panel-by-panel breakdown of your comic, specifying dialogue, captions, action, and sometimes visual angles. In comics, a script can be quite detailed to help the artist.

Typical Contents

Page and Panel Headings: e.g., “PAGE 1 (5 panels). Panel 1: Wide shot of the campsite…”

Action Descriptions: Summaries of what the reader should see in each panel.

Dialogue/Captions: Separated from action, often labeled “Character Name (speech balloon),” or “CAPTION (Greg’s inner thought).”

SFX: Sound effects, placed where needed (“SFX: CRASH!”).

Why You Might Need It

Essential for final production. The artist and letterer rely on it to draw consistent storytelling, place word balloons, and set pacing.

5. Sample Pages / Art Tests
What It Is

Illustrations of selected scenes or “test pages” that show how your script translates visually.

Can include rough pencils, inked pages, colored pages, or fully lettered pages.

Typical Contents

One or Two Key Scenes from your story, showing pivotal moments or showcasing the tone/atmosphere.

Different Settings: e.g., a campsite scene, a biergarten confrontation, a silent disco sequence—so potential collaborators see variety.

Why You Might Need It

If you’re recruiting an artist or pitching to publishers, sample pages demonstrate exactly what the final comic might look like.

Helps refine your style and test if the visuals match the tone in your head.

6. Production Plan / Timeline
What It Is

A simple document outlining who is doing what (writing, penciling, inking, coloring, lettering) and by when.

Typical Contents

Milestones: e.g., “First draft script by Date X,” “Thumbnail sketches by Date Y,” “Inks complete by Date Z,” etc.

Budgets or Payment Plans: If you’re paying an artist or colorist, you might list rates or payment schedule.

Why You Might Need It

Keeps you and any collaborators on track.

Essential if you plan a Kickstarter or any crowdfunded approach (backers like seeing a timeline).

7. Mockup / Layout Document
What It Is

Sometimes creators produce a “layout pass” or “thumbnail pass,” which visually blocks out each comic page in rough form (stick figures, quick shapes for panels).

Typical Contents

Rough Sketches of each page—no polished art, just enough to indicate composition, camera angles, and balloon placements.

Numbered Panels: So the creative team can see how the reading flow works.

Why You Might Need It

Helps preempt layout issues (like too much text in a small panel, or awkward scene transitions).

Speeds up communication with the artist—fewer misunderstandings later on.

8. Marketing & Promotional Materials
What It Is

A package of documents, images, or short text used to promote your comic, either online or at conventions.

Typical Contents

Promo Posters & Banners: Eye-catching visuals referencing your key characters or the festival setting.

Short Teaser Text: “Prepare for the wildest 5-day festival you’ve ever seen…”

Social Media Templates: Mock Instagram posts, Twitter banners, etc.

Why You Might Need It

If you plan to sell or distribute the comic, you’ll want promotional materials—either for a crowdfunding campaign or simply to generate buzz.

Contracts or collaboration agreements between writer, artist, colorist, letterer—ensuring everyone’s on the same page about payment, rights, and timelines.

Typical Contents

Ownership Split: Who owns what percentage of the IP (intellectual property)?
